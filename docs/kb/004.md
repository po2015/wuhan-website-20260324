This article outlines the development and current leading position of China's active electronically scanned array (AESA) radar technology. It explains that unlike traditional mechanically rotating radars, AESA radars are composed of numerous independent Transmit/Receive (T/R) modules, giving them advantages in precise detection, electronic warfare, and multi-platform adaptability. The performance of these T/R modules relies on advanced semiconductor materials, transitioning from Gallium Arsenide (GaAs) to the more powerful Gallium Nitride (GaN).
China's progress is described in three key phases:
Inception through Cooperation (1990s): The collaboration with Israel on the "Phalcon" early warning aircraft project, where China insisted on independently developing the T/R modules, provided a high starting point by tackling the demanding requirements of airborne radar systems.
Breakthrough under Blockade: After external forces terminated the "Phalcon" project and imposed technology embargoes (including on GaAs chips), China was forced into full-chain independent R&D. It first achieved self-sufficiency in GaAs-based T/R module manufacturing and then proactively began developing next-generation GaN technology.
Leading through Comprehensive Application: With core technologies matured, China's AESA radars have been deployed across airborne (e.g., J-20, J-10C fighters), naval (e.g., Type 052D, 055 destroyers), and land-based platforms, forming a comprehensive, multi-domain detection network.
