This article reports on a groundbreaking breakthrough in LiDAR technology, as detailed in a recent publication in Nature Communications.
Key Innovation: Bionic, "Gaze"-enabled FMCW LiDAR
A joint research team from Peking University, City University of Hong Kong, and Shanghai Jiao Tong University has developed a new, integrated bionic Frequency-Modulated Continuous Wave (FMCW) LiDAR architecture. The core inspiration comes from the human visual system, which efficiently combines wide peripheral vision with high-resolution focal "gazing." The new system mimics this by dynamically concentrating its high-resolution sensing resources on key Areas of Interest (AOIs), rather than uniformly scanning the entire field of view. This "micro-parallel" architecture decouples wide-field coverage and high-resolution local imaging, solving the traditional dilemma between resolution, power consumption, and system complexity.
Technical Advancements and Benefits
Architectural Shift: The system moves away from the traditional, costly approach of densely stacking channels across the entire field. Instead, it uses a reconfigurable wavelength/frequency domain to achieve high-resolution "gazing" where needed.
High Performance: The prototype achieves an angular resolution of 0.012° within local AOIs while maintaining large field-of-view coverage.
Enabling Technology: The integration of a thin-film lithium niobate electro-optic frequency comb and a widely tunable external cavity laser enables the dynamic and efficient resource allocation central to the "gazing" capability.
4D-Plus Machine Vision: The system demonstrates real-time parallel 4D (3D spatial + velocity) imaging. Furthermore, by fusing the LiDAR point cloud with data from a visible-light camera, it creates a richer "4D-plus" scene representation, significantly enhancing a machine's understanding of complex environments.
Significance and Future Outlook
This chip-scale, integrated bionic LiDAR offers a new path for next-generation intelligent perception, characterized by high resolution, low power consumption, and high flexibility. It has strong potential for applications in autonomous driving, embodied intelligence, unmanned systems, and integrated air-space-ground networks.
