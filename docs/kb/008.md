This article, titled "From Phased Array to Ultra-Long-Range Detection: A Beginner's Guide to the Core Principles of Radar," provides a foundational explanation of how radar works, with a focus on modern military applications. The summary is structured around four core principles:
Basic Working Logic of Radar: The article explains that radar operates similarly to hearing an echo. It transmits electromagnetic waves (instead of sound waves) and then receives the waves reflected from a target. The "voice box" (transmitter, e.g., a magnetron) and "ear" (receiver) components are described. A key principle is that wavelength determines performance: shorter wavelengths (like X-band) provide higher precision, while longer wavelengths (like S-band) enable longer-range detection. The dual-band radar on the Type 055 destroyer is given as an example that combines both for comprehensive coverage.
Transition from Mechanical to Phased Array Scanning: The article contrasts early mechanical-scanning radars, which physically rotate the antenna, with phased array radars. Phased arrays use the principle of wave interference. By electronically controlling the timing (phase) of waves emitted by an array of many small transmitters, the radar beam can be steered to any direction almost instantly without moving the antenna, achieving much faster, millisecond-level scanning.
Active vs. Passive Phased Array Radar and Core Materials: The key difference between passive (PESA) and active (AESA) phased array radars is explained. In PESA, many transmitters share one large, centralized antenna; a single point of failure can disable the system. In AESA, each transmitter has its own small antenna, making the system far more reliable and capable, as it can continue functioning even if some modules fail. The role of semiconductor materials like Gallium Nitride (GaN) is highlighted. GaN components offer higher power and better heat resistance than older materials like Gallium Arsenide (GaAs), significantly boosting radar range and performance.
Key Challenge for Over-the-Horizon (OTH) Detection: The article addresses the core problem of the Earth's curvature, which blocks line-of-sight radar beams. Two main solutions are presented:
Elevating the Radar: Deploying radar at high altitudes (e.g., on aircraft or mountains) extends the direct line-of-sight detection range. The 7010 radar in China is cited as an example.
Using Atmospheric Reflection (OTH Radar): Systems like the Soviet-era "Woodpecker" (DUGA-3) radar use the ionosphere to bounce powerful radar signals over the horizon, achieving detection ranges of thousands of kilometers, albeit with significant side effects like disrupting global shortwave communications.
The conclusion underscores that the evolution of radar technology—from mechanical scanning to AESA, and from GaAs to GaN—is driven by the constant pursuit of greater range, accuracy, and reliability, which forms the foundation for modern defense capabilities.
