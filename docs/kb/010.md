It can perceive the electromagnetic environment, adapt its waveforms in real-time to optimize detection for different targets (e.g., stealth fighters vs. drones), and avoid interference, significantly improving efficiency and electronic warfare resilience. It describes a trend where LiDAR and millimeter-wave radar are moving towards deep fusion, with LiDAR building the spatial framework and other sensors filling in dynamic information.

In 2025, it began transitioning from lab research to initial commercialization. Key advantages over traditional radars include 10x higher bandwidth (up to 100 GHz), nanosecond-level latency, and a 50%+ reduction in size/weight. Applications include military uses like upgrading fighter jet radars for tracking hypersonic targets and civilian applications like integrated 6G communication/perception base stations for high-precision positioning and next-generation autonomous driving systems.
